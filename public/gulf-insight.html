01 — The Market's Bet
Extreme Backwardation
The market is pricing a short disruption
When futures prices drop sharply for later delivery (backwardation), the market is saying: "This is temporary. Supply will normalize." The prompt spread hit $33 — the most extreme backwardation since the 2008 financial crisis. Normal: $2-3. The market is VERY confident this ends soon.
OVX (oil volatility index) hit 118%. 1-week WTI implied volatility: 171%. The options market is pricing ±11% daily moves.

02 — The Physical Reality
The Infrastructure Says Otherwise
Bypass capacity covers 40% of Hormuz traffic. India has 12 days of reserves.
The market is pricing a return to normal. But the physical infrastructure can't deliver it. Alternative pipelines carry 8.5 million barrels per day — 40% of what Hormuz handled. Strategic reserves are depleted. Asia-Pacific has 52 days of forward cover. India has 12 days in its strategic reserve alone.

03 — The Insurance Signal
The People Who Price Risk for a Living
They are not pricing a short disruption
Marine war risk premiums went from 0.02% to 1-7.5% of hull value in 48 hours. Lloyd's of London redesignated the entire Persian Gulf as a conflict zone. A $250M VLCC now costs $2-15M extra per transit. The insurance market — the market that exists specifically to price catastrophic risk — is pricing this as a prolonged event.

04 — The Historical Precedent
Disruptions End Through Exhaustion, Not Resolution
The Tanker War lasted 4 years. The Suez closures lasted months.
There is no precedent for a Hormuz-scale disruption resolving quickly. The 1980-88 Tanker War: 451 vessel attacks, 4 years, ended when both sides were exhausted. The 1956 Suez Crisis: closed for months. The 1967 Six-Day War closure: 8 years. In every case, the market initially priced a short disruption and was wrong.
Historical strait closures: duration vs market's initial expectation. In every case, actual duration exceeded market pricing by 3-10x.
