# Agentic Economy Editorial Roadmap

Date: 2026-05-06
Status: working editorial plan

## Purpose

The Agentic Economy needs to become a public voice, not just a private research
folder.

This roadmap turns the field map, landscape review, validation guide, and
interview packet into a writing program. The aim is to publish ideas that help
readers see the agentic economy before they need the full doctrine.

The posture:

- Calm.
- Precise.
- Category-forming.
- Not deterministic.
- Not fear-led.
- Not Handshake-first.

## Editorial Standard

Every public piece should do one of four jobs:

1. Name a shift readers can already observe.
2. Explain why existing systems behave strangely under software agency.
3. Map a market or institution being reorganized by agentic action.
4. Show why a new primitive may be required.

Avoid pieces that only:

- Define internal vocabulary.
- Explain Handshake mechanics.
- Predict a totalizing future.
- Argue that every incumbent is wrong.
- Try to sell the company.

## The Spine

The original five essays remain the intellectual spine:

1. Actors without standing.
2. Judgment without context.
3. Scale without organization.
4. Risk without visibility.
5. The hierarchy unanchored.

New writing should not replace them. It should translate them into markets,
operators, and decisions that readers can recognize.

## Editorial Pillars

### Pillar 1: Software Agency

Core question:

> What changes when software stops waiting and begins to act?

Reader:

- Founder.
- Investor.
- Enterprise executive.
- AI infrastructure builder.

Public ideas:

- Software agency is the shift beneath agentic commerce.
- The agentic economy is not a payments category.
- Interfaces recede when software can act directly.
- The useful unit of analysis is the action, not the app.

### Pillar 2: Organizational Change

Core question:

> What happens to the firm when agents reduce coordination costs?

Reader:

- CEO.
- COO.
- CFO.
- Strategy lead.
- Operator.

Public ideas:

- The operator after SaaS.
- The headless firm.
- From seats to actions.
- Approval work becomes policy work.
- Exception handling becomes the human layer.

### Pillar 3: Authority And Proof

Core question:

> What must an agent prove before it is allowed to create consequence?

Reader:

- CISO.
- CIO.
- Compliance lead.
- Platform builder.
- Payment/API/service provider.

Public ideas:

- A working card is not approval.
- A valid API key is not approval.
- A logged-in user is not approval.
- Identity is necessary but insufficient.
- Logs are too late.

### Pillar 4: Market Surfaces

Core question:

> Where does agentic action first meet refusal, risk, and budget?

Reader:

- Investor.
- Founder.
- Product leader.
- Developer platform team.

Public ideas:

- Paid APIs as the first non-commerce market.
- Agentic checkout as proof, not destination.
- SaaS admin actions as the enterprise wedge.
- Agent-to-agent services as the long map.
- The physical world as an endpoint.

### Pillar 5: Infrastructure And Protocols

Core question:

> Which adjacent systems are substrates, which are competitors, and which leave
> the action ceremony open?

Reader:

- Protocol designer.
- Infrastructure founder.
- Technical investor.
- Enterprise architect.

Public ideas:

- A2A lets agents talk. It does not decide.
- MCP grants access. It does not establish business authority.
- Verifiable credentials prove claims. They do not approve actions.
- Crypto settles value. It does not grant authority.
- Payments protocols are inventing mandates because intent matters.

## Publication Sequence

### Series 1: Name The Field

Goal:

- Establish the category without over-explaining.

Pieces:

1. `Software Agency`
   - Claim: the important shift is software that can act.
   - Evidence needed: examples from agents acting across tools, commerce, APIs,
     and enterprise systems.
   - Status: ready after a light source pass.

2. `The Agentic Economy Is Not A Payments Category`
   - Claim: payments are the first obvious consequence, not the whole map.
   - Evidence needed: AP2, ACP, x402, A2A, MCP, enterprise identity movement.
   - Status: supported by landscape review.

3. `The Action Is The Unit`
   - Claim: apps, sessions, users, and credentials are too coarse; the atomic
     business action is where approval matters.
   - Evidence needed: examples from payments, paid APIs, SaaS admin, cloud ops.
   - Status: needs interviews.

### Series 2: The Organization Rewritten

Goal:

- Move from abstract economy to C-suite and operator reality.

Pieces:

1. `The Operator After SaaS`
   - Claim: operators move from clicking through workflows to supervising
     policy, exceptions, and evidence.
   - Evidence needed: operator interviews.
   - Status: not ready.

2. `From Seats To Actions`
   - Claim: agentic work pressures SaaS pricing and workflow design away from
     human seats and toward actions, usage, outcomes, and capabilities.
   - Evidence needed: SaaS vendor/product interviews plus public pricing/API
     examples.
   - Status: needs research.

3. `The Headless Firm`
   - Claim: agents reduce coordination costs and let smaller organizations
     orchestrate broader capability markets.
   - Evidence needed: founder/operator examples and Coase framing.
   - Status: concept ready, evidence thin.

### Series 3: Proof Before Consequence

Goal:

- Articulate the new primitive without making every article about Handshake.

Pieces:

1. `A Working Card Is Not Approval`
   - Claim: payment validity does not prove intent, authority, or consent.
   - Evidence needed: agentic commerce protocols and payment-risk interviews.
   - Status: closest to publishable.

2. `A Valid API Key Is Not Approval`
   - Claim: paid API calls need more than key validity when agents spend or
     access on behalf of principals.
   - Evidence needed: paid API provider interviews.
   - Status: high-priority validation.

3. `A Service Account Is Not Approval`
   - Claim: cloud and enterprise automation need action-level authority, not
     only workload identity.
   - Evidence needed: CISO/cloud platform interviews.
   - Status: later enterprise piece.

4. `Logs Are Too Late`
   - Claim: post-action observability is not enough when agents create
     consequence before review.
   - Evidence needed: risk/compliance/operator interviews.
   - Status: concept ready, evidence needed.

### Series 4: The Market Map

Goal:

- Show the breadth of the agentic economy through concrete surfaces.

Pieces:

1. `Paid APIs And The Agentic Service Market`
   - Claim: paid APIs are early atoms of Business-as-a-Service.
   - Evidence needed: x402 and API provider interviews.
   - Status: high-priority validation.

2. `Agent-To-Agent Is Subcontracting`
   - Claim: when agents hire agents, the economy needs recursive delegation,
     authority, liability, and records.
   - Evidence needed: agent builder interviews.
   - Status: long-term map piece.

3. `The Physical World As An Endpoint`
   - Claim: logistics, labs, robots, manufacturing, and field services turn
     digital authority into physical consequence.
   - Evidence needed: operator interviews in logistics/labs/manufacturing.
   - Status: concept ready, evidence thin.

4. `Crypto Settles Value; It Does Not Grant Authority`
   - Claim: signatures, wallets, and settlement are powerful but do not by
     themselves prove that an action was allowed.
   - Evidence needed: protocol examples and onchain operator interviews.
   - Status: sourceable, should be careful.

## Evidence Gates

### Publishable Without Interviews

Use only for category framing and landscape synthesis.

Requirements:

- Current primary sources.
- Clear uncertainty.
- No claim of buyer pain.
- No claim that "no one is doing this."

Candidates:

- The Agentic Economy Is Not A Payments Category.
- Crypto Settles Value; It Does Not Grant Authority.
- A2A Lets Agents Talk. It Does Not Decide.

### Requires Interviews

Use when claiming urgency, refusal, budget, or workflow pain.

Requirements:

- At least five interviews in the relevant wedge.
- At least three named workflows.
- At least one strong quote.
- Wedge score above 34, or explicit reason for watch-list treatment.

Candidates:

- A Valid API Key Is Not Approval.
- The Operator After SaaS.
- From Seats To Actions.
- A Service Account Is Not Approval.

### Requires Prototype Or Proof Object

Use when claiming a ceremony changes acceptance.

Requirements:

- At least five proof-object reviews.
- At least three interviewees say the object would improve acceptance.
- Clear field-level feedback.

Candidates:

- What An Agent Must Prove Before It Acts.
- Proof Before Consequence.
- The First Ceremony For Agentic Operations.

## Editorial Voice Rules

Do:

- Begin with an observable shift.
- Use short, declarative claims.
- Show systems under pressure.
- Preserve uncertainty.
- Distinguish identity, payment, access, approval, and record.
- Let readers interpolate.

Do not:

- Overuse "trust layer."
- Lead with fear.
- Force Handshake into every piece.
- Make "agentic economy" do all the work.
- Claim inevitability where we only have direction.
- Spell out the whole thesis every time.

## First Four Public Pieces

### 1. The Agentic Economy Is Not A Payments Category

Purpose:

- Establish breadth without dismissing payments.

Argument:

- Payments are where consequence becomes obvious.
- But agentic action also moves through APIs, SaaS, data, cloud, service
  markets, and physical operations.
- The field is software agency, not checkout automation.

Evidence:

- Landscape review.
- AP2, ACP, x402, A2A, MCP, enterprise identity movement.

Status:

- Can draft now.

### 2. A Working Card Is Not Approval

Purpose:

- Explain Handshake's first wedge without over-explaining the product.

Argument:

- A payment method proves that money can move.
- It does not prove the principal intended this action, authorized this agent,
  or accepted this counterparty and limit.
- Agentic commerce needs approval before action.

Evidence:

- Payment protocol landscape.
- Payment-risk interviews needed before strong market claims.

Status:

- Draft after three payment/counterparty interviews.

### 3. A Valid API Key Is Not Approval

Purpose:

- Open the strongest adjacent market.

Argument:

- Paid APIs are becoming callable business services.
- API keys identify access and billing accounts.
- Agentic API calls need action-specific authority when spend, data, or resale
  risk exists.

Evidence:

- x402 landscape.
- Paid API provider interviews.

Status:

- Draft after paid API interview cluster.

### 4. The Operator After SaaS

Purpose:

- Make the transition legible at the human workflow level.

Argument:

- Operators used to move work through software.
- Agents move the work.
- Operators become policy authors, exception handlers, and evidence reviewers.

Evidence:

- Operator interviews.
- Support, finance, procurement, cloud operations examples.

Status:

- Not ready until interviews.

## Research-To-Writing Loop

After every four interviews:

1. Add the strongest quote to the evidence log.
2. Identify the most resonant wedge sentence.
3. Decide whether any essay moved from "concept" to "draftable."
4. Update the roadmap status.

After twelve interviews:

1. Pick one wedge for deep dive.
2. Pick one public essay.
3. Pick one proof-object memo.
4. Decide whether the homepage/about copy should change.

## Success Criteria

The editorial program is working if:

- Readers can explain the category without repeating product copy.
- Each essay adds one clear concept to the map.
- Handshake reads as a natural artifact of the research.
- Investors see market breadth.
- Builders see infrastructure opportunity.
- Operators recognize their workflows.
- Risk owners see why existing controls may be insufficient.

The editorial program is failing if:

- Every piece sounds like a product pitch.
- The language becomes foreboding.
- The map collapses into payments.
- The map collapses into crypto.
- The map collapses into identity.
- The site explains too much and says too little.
