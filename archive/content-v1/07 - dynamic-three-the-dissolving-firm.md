# The Dissolving Firm

**Part 7 of the Agentic Economy Series**

The first two dynamics addressed the agent as actor and decision-maker. This dynamic addresses the agent as institution.

Organizations exist to solve problems that individuals cannot solve alone. Coordination, specialization, scale. A firm can do what a person cannot: marshal resources, divide labor, persist knowledge, amortize fixed costs across many transactions. The institutional advantage is the ability to coordinate human activity at scale.

Software has been eroding that advantage for decades. Email replaced the mailroom. Spreadsheets replaced the accounting department. Databases replaced the filing cabinet. But the erosion was partial. Software automated tasks. It did not replicate the organizational capacity for coordination and judgment.

Agents change that. The capabilities that required organizations — coordination across domains, judgment under uncertainty, adaptive response to changing conditions — are becoming possible in software. An agent can manage a sales pipeline, coordinate marketing campaigns, handle customer support, analyze financial data, and adjust strategy based on outcomes. Not because it has been programmed for each task, but because it can reason, plan, and execute toward goals.

The institutional advantage is no longer institutional. It is computational.

This is not about replacing workers. It is about replacing the function of the firm. A single individual, augmented by agents, can operate at the scale that previously required a team. The organizational layer — the structure that coordinated human effort — becomes software. The firm dissolves into tools.

Consider what a small business does. Marketing, sales, operations, finance, customer support. Each function requires knowledge, judgment, and coordination with other functions. Traditionally, this meant hiring people. Each person brought skills, required management, introduced coordination costs. The firm grew to handle the complexity.

Now consider what an individual with agents can do. One agent manages outreach and qualification. Another handles customer support tickets. A third monitors financial metrics and flags anomalies. A fourth coordinates between them, ensuring that customer support insights feed into marketing, that sales data informs operations. The coordination layer is software. The judgment layer is software. The individual provides direction and makes the decisions that matter.

The firm has not been replaced. It has been concentrated. The organizational structure that required hierarchy, departments, meetings, and management is compressed into tools. The overhead of coordination — the friction that justified the firm's existence — is eliminated.

This has implications for employment, but the deeper implication is for economic structure. The firm exists because it is more efficient than the alternative: contracting for every service in the market. Transaction costs — the expense of finding, negotiating, and enforcing agreements — made hierarchy cheaper than the market. Agents reduce transaction costs toward zero. Finding a service becomes search. Negotiating becomes specification. Enforcement becomes logging and verification.

When transaction costs approach zero, the advantage of the firm diminishes. The market becomes viable at smaller scales. The individual, equipped with agents, can access capabilities that previously required organizational scale. The firm does not disappear. But it becomes optional for a larger set of activities.

The institutional layer is not just changing. It is liquefying. The solid structures that organized economic activity — departments, roles, hierarchies — become fluid. The individual assembles capabilities rather than joining organizations. The organization becomes one option among many for structuring economic activity.

This is the third structural gap. The first was actors without standing. The second was judgment without context. This is scale without organization. The capabilities of the firm, available to the individual, without the institutional structure that made them governable.
