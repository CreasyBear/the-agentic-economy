# The New Oligopolies

**Part 9 of the Agentic Economy Series**

The previous pieces established that institutional capability is concentrating into software, and that the surplus is split between individuals and infrastructure providers. This piece examines the structure of that infrastructure layer.

The agents that enable individual scale depend on models, platforms, and tools. Each layer is concentrated.

Models: A handful of organizations — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, a few others — train the frontier models that power agents. Training requires compute, data, and capital at scales that exclude most participants. The models themselves are differentiated. GPT-5 is not Claude is not Gemini. The capabilities are not interchangeable. The model layer is an oligopoly with high barriers to entry and significant differentiation between providers.

Platforms: The infrastructure for deploying, orchestrating, and managing agents is also concentrated. Cloud providers, agent frameworks, integration platforms — each category has a small number of dominant players. The platform layer provides the middleware between models and applications. It captures value through usage-based pricing, subscription fees, and ecosystem lock-in.

Tools: The interfaces that connect agents to the world — APIs, databases, communication channels — are controlled by the platforms they integrate with. An agent that sends email depends on email providers. An agent that processes payments depends on payment processors. An agent that accesses data depends on data sources. The tool layer is fragmented but gatekept. Each integration is controlled by its provider.

This structure creates a new form of economic power. The infrastructure layer sits between individuals and their ability to operate at scale. Access to that infrastructure determines who can participate in the agent economy and on what terms.

The concentration is not inherently problematic. Infrastructure has always tended toward concentration — railroads, telecommunications, utilities. The question is how the concentration is governed and what alternatives exist.

In previous infrastructure transitions, the response included:
- **Regulation:** Utilities were regulated as natural monopolies. Rates were controlled. Service obligations were imposed. The concentration was accepted, but the terms were set by public authority.
- **Competition policy:** Antitrust action broke up concentrations that were deemed harmful to competition. Standard Oil, AT&T, the railroads. The market was restructured to create alternatives.
- **Public provision:** Some infrastructure was built and operated by government. Roads, water systems, the internet itself in its early stages. The infrastructure was owned collectively.
- **Open alternatives:** Some infrastructure developed open standards and decentralized provision. The internet protocols, open-source software. The concentration was avoided through design.

The agent infrastructure layer is forming now. The governance choices are being made — or not made — in real time. The current trajectory favors private concentration with minimal regulation. The leading model providers are racing to build capability and capture market share. The platforms are building moats through ecosystem lock-in. The tool providers are controlling access through API terms and rate limits.

This creates risk. If the infrastructure layer remains concentrated and ungoverned, the distribution of surplus will favor infrastructure providers. Individuals will have sovereignty but not bargaining power. The dissolution of the firm will have concentrated economic power rather than democratized it.

It also creates opportunity. Open models, decentralized platforms, and community-owned infrastructure are alternatives. They require capital, coordination, and sustained effort. But they are possible. The infrastructure layer is not yet locked in. The choices made in the next few years will shape it for decades.

The third dynamic — institutional disruption — is not just about the firm dissolving. It is about what replaces it. The infrastructure that enables individual scale will determine whether that scale is genuinely empowering or merely dependent.
