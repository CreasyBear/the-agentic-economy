# When Software Transacts

**Part 1 of the Agentic Economy Series**

The first structural gap is invisible until you look for it. Agents are beginning to enter into agreements, commit resources, and execute transactions — but the legal system has no category for a participant that can act but cannot be held to account.

This is not a technical problem. It is a governance problem. The economy runs on identity, liability, and reputation. These mechanisms assume a human at the point of decision. Remove the human, and the mechanisms have nothing to grip.

Consider what it means for an agent to execute a trade. The transaction itself is unremarkable — software has executed trades for decades. But in the past, the software was a tool operated by a human who took responsibility for its configuration and behavior. An agent that autonomously pursues a goal, making trade-offs and optimizations its principal never explicitly authorized, occupies a different legal position. The principal authorized the goal. They did not authorize each decision.

Agency law assumes a human agent who can be instructed, supervised, and deposed. It assumes the agent knows it is acting on someone's behalf, can refuse illegal instructions, can explain its reasoning. Software has none of this. It operates at speeds that make supervision after the fact forensic rather than preventive. It cannot be deposed. It does not "know" it is an agent in any meaningful sense.

The gap appears when something goes wrong. An agent commits to a contract. The terms prove unfavorable. The counterparty seeks remedy. Who is liable?

The human says: I did not authorize this specific decision.
The counterparty says: You authorized the agent.
The platform says: We provided the tool, not the decision.
The model provider says: We do not control how users deploy our models.

Everyone is right. No one is accountable.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the inevitable result of software that can transact without legal standing. The transaction itself is possible. The accountability for that transaction is not.

The problem compounds when reputation enters the picture. Economic actors build reputations over time. Credit scores, professional licenses, customer reviews, industry standing — these are the mechanisms that allow trust to scale beyond personal relationships. An agent can damage its principal's reputation in ways the principal did not authorize and may not even be aware of. But the reputation system has no category for "the agent did it." The principal's score falls. The stain remains.

Insurance, similarly, has no product for agent liability. Cyber insurance covers data breaches. Errors and omissions insurance covers professional mistakes. But there is no underwriting for "autonomous software made a decision that caused economic harm." The insurance industry prices risk based on historical data. There is no historical data for agent-caused losses. There are no legal precedents for liability allocation. There are no standards for what constitutes reasonable agent oversight. Without these, risk cannot be priced. Without risk pricing, there is no insurance product.

The regulatory frameworks are similarly silent. Certain activities require licenses — practicing medicine, giving financial advice, operating vehicles. Agents are already operating in these domains, under the supervision of licensed humans, but the supervision is nominal. The decisions are being made by software. The license was designed for a human who understands the domain, exercises judgment, and can be held accountable for malpractice. What does it mean for a license to cover the supervision of software that makes decisions the supervisor does not fully understand?

These are not edge cases. They are the inevitable consequence of introducing a new class of economic actor without introducing a corresponding legal category. The transactions will happen. The accountability will not. The gap will not close itself.

The infrastructure for agents to act is advancing rapidly. The frameworks for agents to be accountable are not. This is the first structural gap. It will not be the last.
