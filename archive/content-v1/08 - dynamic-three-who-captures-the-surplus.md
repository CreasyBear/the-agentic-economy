# Who Captures the Surplus

**Part 8 of the Agentic Economy Series**

The previous piece established that institutional capability is concentrating into software. This piece asks where the value goes.

When an individual with agents can operate at the scale of a firm, productivity increases. More output per person. The question is who benefits from that productivity gain.

The historical pattern is mixed. Industrialization increased productivity dramatically. The benefits were distributed — unevenly — between capital owners, workers (eventually, after struggle), and consumers (through lower prices). The institution of the firm mediated this distribution. Wages, profits, prices — these were determined within a framework of labor markets, capital markets, and competition.

The dissolution of the firm changes the mediation. There is no longer an organizational structure that balances the interests of labor and capital. The individual is both. But the infrastructure they depend on — the models, the platforms, the tools — is owned by someone else.

Consider the economics. An individual uses agents to deliver services that previously required a team. Revenue flows to the individual. But costs flow to the infrastructure providers: the model API, the platform hosting, the tool subscriptions. The individual captures the marginal value of their direction and judgment. The infrastructure captures the value of the capability layer.

This creates a new concentration. The tools that enable individual scale are owned by a small number of providers. The models that power agents are built by fewer organizations still. The individual has sovereignty over their work, but dependency on their infrastructure. The surplus generated by productivity gains is split between the individual operator and the infrastructure provider.

The split matters. If the infrastructure captures most of the surplus, then the dissolution of the firm does not democratize economic power. It concentrates it in a different form. The individual becomes more productive but not more prosperous. The value flows upward to the providers of capability.

The terms of this split are not fixed. They are determined by competition, regulation, and the bargaining power of individuals relative to infrastructure providers. If agents are commoditized — if many providers offer equivalent capability — then individuals retain more surplus. If agents are differentiated — if capability depends on a small number of proprietary systems — then infrastructure captures more.

The current trajectory favors concentration. Model training requires massive capital. The leading models are built by a handful of organizations. The tools that integrate models into workflows are also concentrated. The infrastructure layer is not competitive in the classical sense. It is an oligopoly forming in real time.

Employment effects compound the distribution question. If individuals can operate at firm scale, what happens to the people who would have been hired? Some become individual operators themselves. Some become infrastructure providers. Some are displaced. The aggregate productivity gain does not automatically translate to aggregate prosperity. The distribution depends on who can access the tools, who can afford the infrastructure, who has the skills to direct agents effectively.

This creates a divergence. Those who can effectively leverage agents pull ahead. They capture more surplus, operate at larger scale, compound their advantage. Those who cannot are displaced or marginalized. The inequality is not just income. It is capability. Access to agents becomes access to institutional scale. Without that access, the individual is constrained to individual scale — a fraction of what agents make possible.

The firm, for all its flaws, provided a distribution mechanism. Wages, benefits, career paths, training. The dissolution of the firm removes that mechanism without replacing it. The individual is sovereign but isolated. The market is fluid but unforgiving. The infrastructure is powerful but owned by others.

This is not an argument for preserving the firm. The institutional advantage is eroding regardless. It is an observation about what comes next: a shift in how value is created, and an open question about how it is distributed.
