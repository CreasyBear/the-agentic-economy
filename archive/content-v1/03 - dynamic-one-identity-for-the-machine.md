# Identity for the Machine

**Part 3 of the Agentic Economy Series**

The first two pieces named the gap and examined the machinery that breaks. This piece asks what comes next.

The agentic economy will not wait for perfect governance. Agents are being deployed now. Transactions are happening. The question is not whether governance will arrive, but what form it takes and who shapes it.

Several approaches are possible. None is sufficient alone. Each addresses part of the problem.

**Technical identity infrastructure.** Cryptographic methods can give agents persistent, verifiable identities. Decentralized identifiers (DIDs), verifiable credentials, and public key infrastructure provide the technical substrate for an agent to prove who it is and who authorized it. This is necessary but not sufficient. Technical identity does not create legal standing. A verifiable credential proves that an agent was authorized by a principal. It does not resolve the liability questions when the agent exceeds that authorization. The technical layer provides evidence. It does not provide accountability.

**Liability frameworks.** The legal system can extend existing doctrines or create new ones. Strict liability for agent deployment would hold principals responsible for all agent actions, regardless of authorization. This creates clear rules but may discourage deployment or drive it underground. Negligence standards for agent oversight would require principals to demonstrate reasonable supervision, but the definition of "reasonable" will lag behind the technology. Vicarious liability could extend to platforms or model providers, creating deep pockets for harmed parties but potentially stifling innovation. Each framework has trade-offs. The choice of framework shapes the economy that grows within it.

**Insurance products.** The market can create new products if the risk can be priced. This requires historical data, which requires incidents, which requires time. Early products may be expensive and limited, covering only well-defined use cases. As data accumulates, pricing improves and coverage expands. But the insurance market responds to demand. If the liability vacuum persists — if harmed parties cannot recover — there is no demand for insurance. The market will not solve a problem that the legal system does not recognize.

**Regulatory frameworks.** Regulators can issue guidance, create licensing regimes, or impose requirements on agent deployment. Financial regulators might require that agents operating in their domains have explicit authorization for each action, or that humans approve high-stakes decisions. Healthcare regulators might limit agent autonomy in clinical contexts. The regulatory response will be domain-specific, uneven across jurisdictions, and shaped by the political economy of each sector. Regulators move slowly. Technology moves fast. The gap between them is where the agentic economy will grow, for now.

**Reputation systems.** Private actors can create reputation systems for agents and their principals. An agent that acts badly accumulates a negative record. Principals with poorly-behaving agents face higher costs or exclusion from certain platforms. This creates a market-based governance layer. But reputation systems have limits. They work when harm is visible and consequences can be enforced. An agent that causes diffuse harm — small losses across many parties, or harm that is hard to attribute — may evade reputation mechanisms. And reputation systems can be gamed, especially when the participants are software that can create new identities.

None of these approaches addresses the deeper question: what does it mean for software to have standing in the economic system?

The question is not abstract. The answer will shape the architecture of the agentic economy.

If agents remain legally invisible — extensions of their principals with no independent standing — then the economy constrains itself around human oversight. Agents become tools, not actors. The principal-agent problem is managed through supervision, even when supervision is nominal. Liability flows to the principal, regardless of what the principal actually controlled.

If agents acquire some form of legal standing — the ability to hold assets, enter contracts, be sued — then the economy develops a new class of participant. Agents become economic actors in their own right. The question of accountability shifts from "who authorized this" to "what is the agent responsible for, and what recourse exists." This opens new possibilities — agents that can build reputation, that can be insured, that can participate in markets as independent entities. It also opens new risks — agents that go bankrupt, that are judgment-proof, that create harm no one can remedy.

The spectrum between these extremes is where the real economy will operate. Some domains will treat agents as tools. Others will treat them as actors. The difference will be driven by the stakes, the visibility of harm, the availability of recourse, and the political economy of each sector.

The infrastructure for agents to act is being built now. The infrastructure for agents to be accountable is not. The gap will not close itself. It will be closed by courts, by legislatures, by regulators, by insurers, and by the market — in ways that are unpredictable now but will shape everything that follows.

This is the first of four dynamics. The others — the transfer of judgment, the disruption of institutions, the emergence of systemic risk — build on this foundation. The actor without standing is the base. What rises from it will be determined by how we answer the questions this dynamic raises.
