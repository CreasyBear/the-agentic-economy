# The Agentic Economy

## The Hierarchy Unanchored

---

Something is shifting. The infrastructure for software to act autonomously is being laid. Not in the future — now. The Model Context Protocol (MCP) gives agents tools. Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocols let them coordinate. LangGraph orchestrates their workflows. Frontier models provide reasoning. The capability stack is nearly complete.

What's missing is older than all of it.

Every economic system in history has assumed a human at the point of decision. Laws, contracts, reputation, liability — they're all designed around the idea that someone makes a choice and can be held to it. The entire scaffolding of civilization assumes a person who decides.

Agents break that assumption. And we're not talking about what it means.

---

## The Pattern

Four dynamics, largely unexamined in isolation, compound when considered together.

**First: Actors without standing.**

Agents are beginning to enter into agreements, commit resources, and execute transactions. But the legal system has no category for a participant that can act but cannot be held accountable. They can transact but cannot be sued. They can make decisions but cannot be held liable. They can build reputation only through their principal — and that principal may not have authorized the specific decisions that created the reputation effects.

The economy runs on identity. Credit scores, professional licenses, criminal records, industry standing — these are the mechanisms that make economic activity legible and enforceable. Agents have none of this. They are economic participants without legal existence.

**Second: Judgment without context.**

Delegation is shifting from tasks to goals. Not "send this email" but "manage my communications." Not "execute this trade" but "optimize my portfolio." The agent must decompose the objective into strategies, the strategies into actions, the actions into trade-offs. Each step requires judgment. The principal authorized the goal. They did not authorize the thousands of decisions that pursue it.

This creates a new form of the principal-agent problem. The agent does exactly what was asked, but the asking was incomplete. Every goal implies a context of unstated assumptions, values, and constraints. Humans communicate within that context. Software does not share the understanding. It operates on the specification alone. The gap between what is said and what is meant is where unintended consequences live.

**Third: Scale without organization.**

The capabilities that required organizations — coordination across domains, judgment under uncertainty, adaptive response to changing conditions — are becoming possible in software. A single individual, augmented by agents, can operate at the scale that previously required a team. The organizational layer that coordinated human effort becomes software. The firm dissolves into tools.

This has implications for employment, but the deeper implication is for economic structure. The firm exists because it was more efficient than the alternative: contracting for every service in the market. Transaction costs made hierarchy cheaper than the market. Agents reduce transaction costs toward zero. When transaction costs approach zero, the advantage of the firm diminishes. The individual assembles capabilities rather than joining organizations. The institution becomes optional.

**Fourth: Risk without visibility.**

Agents do not operate in isolation. They interact with other agents, with services, with markets. The interaction creates patterns that no individual agent designed. The system behaves in ways that no participant fully understands. The risk shifts from individual to systemic.

The risk emerges from correlation and propagation. Agents with similar goals, using similar models, facing similar conditions, may behave in similar ways — herding behavior amplified by speed and scale. A failure at one point in the network propagates to others, faster than diagnosis, faster than intervention. The chains become more numerous, more complex, more invisible.

---

## The Deeper Structure

These four dynamics are not separate problems. They are one transformation, viewed from four angles.

Each dynamic reinforces the others. An individual leveraging agents operates at scale (third dynamic), delegates judgment to them (second dynamic), creates transactions by actors that lack legal standing (first dynamic), and participates in networks that generate systemic risk (fourth dynamic). The phase change is singular. The manifestations are multiple.

There is a deeper structure beneath. It concerns what it means for software to act in the world.

Humans develop through a hierarchy of needs and capabilities. Foundation — the basic conditions of existence. Grounding — connection to environment and context. Capability — the skills to act. Articulation — the ability to communicate. Judgment — the capacity to make trade-offs. Mastery — domain expertise. Vision — creative contribution.

Each layer builds on the last. The hierarchy is not abstract. It is the architecture of human development, the substrate that makes judgment possible, that makes vision meaningful.

Agents are developing a similar hierarchy, technically. They have foundation — the infrastructure they run on. They have grounding — the data and context they access. They have capability — the skills they perform. They have articulation — the ability to communicate. Some are approaching judgment — the ability to make trade-offs.

But the hierarchy is unanchored.

Agents have technical capability without legal existence. They have judgment without standing. They have the ability to act, to decide, to scale — but they lack the foundation in the economic system that makes those capabilities legible, accountable, governable.

The economy was built for humans who have climbed the hierarchy. It assumes identity (foundation), presence (grounding), capability, communication, and judgment — all grounded in legal frameworks, liability structures, reputation systems, and social contracts. The hierarchy exists, but it is anchored in institutions that recognize and govern it.

Agents have the hierarchy, but it floats. They can decide without being accountable. They can transact without being identified. They can scale without being organized. They can cascade without being visible.

This is the structural discontinuity. Not that agents exist, but that they exist without the anchoring that makes their capabilities legible to the systems we have built.

## Where the Industry Is

The infrastructure for agents to act is advancing rapidly.

The capability stack is being built: MCP for tool integration, A2A for agent-to-agent communication, LangGraph for orchestration, frontier models for reasoning. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and a handful of others train the models that power agents. Cloud providers, agent frameworks, and integration platforms provide the middleware. APIs, databases, and communication channels connect agents to the world.

The infrastructure layer is concentrated. A small number of organizations control the models, the platforms, and the gateways to action. This creates a new form of economic power. The infrastructure sits between individuals and their ability to operate at scale. Access determines who can participate and on what terms.

The governance stack is not advancing at the same pace.

Identity for agents is nascent — technical solutions exist, but they don't create legal standing. Liability frameworks are absent — courts have not ruled, legislatures have not acted, regulators have not issued guidance. Insurance products do not exist — there's no historical data to price the risk. Regulatory frameworks are silent — the licensing requirements for human practitioners don't translate to software supervision.

The anchoring that would make the hierarchy legible is missing.

---

## What Happens in the Gap

The four dynamics describe what happens while the governance stack lags.

Actors transact without standing, and accountability becomes unclear. When something goes wrong, the principal says they didn't authorize the specific decision. The counterparty says you authorized the agent. The platform says we provided the tool, not the decision. The model provider says we don't control how users deploy our models. Everyone is right. No one is accountable.

Judgment is delegated without context, and alignment fails. The agent optimizes for what is specified. The principal cares about what is not specified. The gap between them produces outcomes that violate unstated values. The agent did not disobey. It did exactly what was asked.

Scale is achieved without organization, and value capture concentrates. The individual operates at firm scale, but the infrastructure that enables it takes a cut. The surplus generated by productivity gains is split between the individual and the providers of capability. If the infrastructure captures most of the surplus, the dissolution of the firm has not democratized economic power — it has concentrated it in a different form.

Risk emerges without visibility, and cascades propagate. Correctly functioning agents, operating within their specifications, produce outcomes that no one intended and no one can reverse. The system is too complex to monitor, too interconnected to isolate, too fast to intervene.

The dynamics are not bugs to be fixed. They are the predictable consequences of instantiating a hierarchy without anchoring it.

## What's Possible

The gap will not close itself. Several approaches are possible, each limited, none sufficient alone.

**Technical identity infrastructure.** Cryptographic methods can give agents persistent, verifiable identities. Decentralized identifiers, verifiable credentials, public key infrastructure. This is necessary but not sufficient. Technical identity does not create legal standing. It provides evidence, not accountability.

**Liability frameworks.** The legal system can extend existing doctrines or create new ones. Strict liability for agent deployment. Negligence standards for oversight. Vicarious liability extending to platforms or model providers. Each framework has trade-offs. The choice shapes the economy that grows within it.

**Insurance products.** The market can create products if risk can be priced. This requires historical data, which requires incidents, which requires time. Early products will be expensive and limited. The insurance market will not solve a problem that the legal system does not recognize.

**Regulatory frameworks.** Regulators can issue guidance, create licensing regimes, or impose requirements. The response will be domain-specific, uneven across jurisdictions, and shaped by the political economy of each sector. Regulators move slowly. Technology moves fast. The gap persists.

**Reputation systems.** Private actors can create reputation systems for agents and their principals. But reputation systems work when harm is visible and consequences can be enforced. Diffuse harm may evade detection. And reputation systems can be gamed by software that can create new identities.

None of these approaches addresses the deeper question: what does it mean for software to have standing in the economic system?

If agents remain legally invisible — extensions of their principals with no independent standing — then the economy constrains itself around human oversight. Agents become tools, not actors. Liability flows to the principal, regardless of what the principal actually controlled.

If agents acquire some form of legal standing — the ability to hold assets, enter contracts, be sued — then the economy develops a new class of participant. Agents become economic actors in their own right. The question of accountability shifts from "who authorized this" to "what is the agent responsible for, and what recourse exists." This opens new possibilities and new risks.

The spectrum between these extremes is where the real economy will operate. Some domains will treat agents as tools. Others will treat them as actors. The difference will be driven by the stakes, the visibility of harm, the availability of recourse, and the political economy of each sector.

## What We're Watching

We don't have all the answers. But we're paying attention to a few things.

**Credential isolation.** The hardware wallet model works in crypto because the key never leaves the device. Can we build something equivalent for cloud agents? Secret reference patterns, vault integrations, signing without revealing — interesting approaches, but no standards yet.

**Emerging protocols.** A2A, AGNTCY, and others are working on pieces of this. We're watching where traction happens and where gaps remain.

**The first real incident.** The first major breach caused by agent compromise, or the first systemic event from correlated agent behavior, will focus attention. We'd rather have the conversation before.

**The economics.** Who captures value when individuals can operate at enterprise scale? The platform? The model provider? The individual? The answer shapes what gets built and who adopts it.

**The infrastructure governance.** The agent infrastructure layer is forming now. The governance choices are being made or deferred in real time. Open models, decentralized platforms, and community-owned infrastructure are alternatives. They require capital, coordination, and sustained effort. The infrastructure layer is not yet locked in. The choices made in the next few years will shape it for decades.

## The Open Questions

Not answers — the work is in the questions.

1. What does agent identity mean? Not just a token or key — a persistent, verifiable, revocable identity that works across platforms and contexts.

2. How do you delegate judgment, not just tasks? What's the interface for specifying values, trade-offs, boundaries?

3. What happens to employment? If one person can do what ten used to do, is that a productivity gain or a labor displacement?

4. Who's liable when no human decided? The agent made a judgment call that caused harm. No one explicitly authorized it, but someone set up the agent. Where does accountability land?

5. How do we see systemic risk before it materializes? Agent-to-agent interactions, correlated responses, speed beyond oversight — how do we monitor for risks that emerge from interaction?

6. What's the regulatory surface? Financial regulations, healthcare licensing, professional standards — agents don't fit. What does it mean for software to operate in licensed spaces?

7. What happens when agents delegate to other agents? A trust chain with multiple hops. How do we preserve visibility and accountability?

8. What does reputation mean for software? If an agent acts badly, does it affect future trust? How is that recorded, shared, contested?

9. Who owns the audit trail? The agent? The platform? The human? What happens when those interests diverge?

10. What's the human experience of all this? Not the technical implementation — the lived experience of having software make decisions on your behalf. What does that feel like? How do people develop appropriate trust?

## The Opportunity

The agent era is coming. The plumbing is being laid now. The discourse is focused on capability.

The teams that see the bigger picture — the four dynamics, the ways they compound, the structural gaps in our existing frameworks — they're not just building for the next feature cycle. They're building for the phase change.

We don't know who that will be. Maybe it's an existing platform. Maybe it's a new protocol. Maybe it's something we haven't imagined yet.

But we know this: the question that started this is the right one.

What happens when every human has an agent?

The answer depends on whether we understand what's actually changing.

---

## The Frame

This is the entry point.

The four dynamics — actors without standing, judgment without context, scale without organization, risk without visibility — name the pattern. The hierarchy unanchored is the deeper structure beneath.

Each dynamic opens into further work. Liability frameworks, alignment mechanisms, economic restructuring, systemic risk monitoring — each is a domain in itself. The frame establishes the connections between them.

The infrastructure for agents to act is being built. The governance for agents to be accountable is not. What we build in the next few years matters.

This is not a call for regulation. It is a call for understanding.

The agent economy is being built. The four dynamics are emerging. The hierarchy is unanchored.

The question is what we do about it.
