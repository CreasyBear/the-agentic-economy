## I. The Promise Nobody Tested

Perry Mehrling, the economist whose "hierarchy of money" framework maps how financial systems actually work, identified four prices of money. The most fundamental is par — the promise that one form of money converts to another at face value. Your bank deposit equals cash, one for one. A money market fund share trades at $1. A gold futures contract can be exchanged for physical gold.

Par seems trivially obvious. Until it isn't.

The entire derivatives market — trillions of dollars in futures, options, swaps — rests on par. The paper price of gold, silver, oil, wheat is considered "the price" because the system assumes that paper claims convert to physical reality when called upon. If that assumption holds, the paper market is a perfectly adequate proxy for the physical market. If it breaks, the two markets diverge — and the paper price becomes a fiction that fewer and fewer participants believe.

For decades, par held in precious metals because almost nobody tested it. COMEX — the Commodity Exchange, part of CME Group — is where gold and silver futures trade. It is also where futures buyers can turn their contracts into physical metal through delivery. The delivery mechanism existed. It worked. But it was almost never used. Through the 2010s, less than 1% of contracts resulted in physical delivery. The other 99% were cash-settled or rolled forward. The paper price *was* the price because everyone agreed it was.

In 2025, the agreement started to dissolve.

## II. Two Different Drains

Before examining the data, a distinction is necessary. The vault drain in gold and the vault drain in silver are not the same phenomenon. They share a surface-level similarity — physical metal leaving exchange warehouses — but the actors, causes, and implications differ.

The silver drain is primarily industrial. Solar panel manufacturing, electronics, and electric vehicles consume silver at rates that mine supply cannot match. The Silver Institute projects the sixth consecutive structural deficit in 2026 at 67 million ounces. The cumulative deficit since 2021 approaches 800 million ounces — nearly one full year of global mine production drawn from above-ground stocks. China's Shanghai Futures Exchange inventories have fallen to decade lows. India added 40 million ounces to ETFs in two months. The drain is real, but it is driven by physical consumption, not monetary demand.

The gold drain is monetary. Central banks are accumulating gold at rates not seen since the 1960s — over 1,080 tonnes in 2022, 850+ tonnes annually since. This is not consumption. Gold is not burned, soldered, or embedded in circuit boards. It sits in vaults. Its purpose is reserve diversification — a shift from paper claims to physical metal, driven by a calculation that changed in February 2022.

This piece focuses on gold. The silver data is mentioned where it illustrates broader market stress, but the structural argument is about gold as a reserve asset. Conflating the two would obscure the mechanism rather than illuminate it.

## III. The Numbers

With that distinction established, the gold data.

**Delivery rates surged.** In 2025, approximately 7% of COMEX gold open interest resulted in physical delivery. The historical baseline was below 1%. October 2025 saw 197.5 tonnes settled — the largest active-month delivery on record. December 2025 processed 115.1 tonnes. Every single contract month in 2025 set a delivery record.

**The vaults are contracting.** COMEX gold warehouse stocks peaked at 43.3 million ounces in March 2025 after a tariff-driven arbitrage inflow. By December 2025, they had declined to approximately 39 million ounces — a 10% reduction. More critically, registered inventory (metal with warrants attached, available for immediate delivery) compressed from an estimated 13.5 million ounces in March to 10.1 million by year-end. Deliverable supply contracted by 25% even as prices hit record highs.

**Central banks are the buyers.** This is the data point that distinguishes 2025–2026 from every previous COMEX scare. The buyers are not retail speculators. They are sovereign entities. Over 3,200 tonnes of cumulative central bank purchases since 2022. By mid-2026, global central bank gold reserves exceeded their US Treasury holdings for the first time in three decades. BRICS+ nations now hold 17.4% of global gold reserves, up from 11.2% in 2019. Gold has overtaken the euro as the world's second-most important reserve asset.

These are not the actions of a community trying to squeeze a short position. They are the actions of sovereign entities restructuring their reserves in response to a specific event.

## IV. Why This Time

For fifteen years, precious metals analysts warned about COMEX delivery failures. For fifteen years, the system held. The warnings were not wrong in logic — they were wrong in participants. The retail "drain COMEX" movement was real in intent but small in scale. A few thousand individuals buying silver eagles does not move a market built for institutional flows.

What changed in 2022 was not the retail demand. It was the institutional calculation.

On February 26, 2022, Western nations froze approximately $300 billion in Russian foreign exchange reserves held in Western institutions. The assets were not seized. They were frozen — inaccessible to the Russian central bank, but technically still belonging to Russia. The distinction mattered less than the signal: sovereign reserves held in another country's financial system could be neutralized by political decision.

Zoltan Pozsar, then at Credit Suisse, published a series of dispatches in March 2022 describing what he called the birth of "Bretton Woods III." His core argument was precise: the freezing of Russian reserves introduced "confiscation risk" to assets previously considered risk-free. Inside money — Treasuries, bank deposits, central bank reserves held at Western institutions — now carried a risk premium that had not existed before. The response, he predicted, would be a structural shift toward outside money: commodities, gold, physical assets that no government could freeze.

The data confirms the prediction. Not in the way retail commentators expected — COMEX delivery failures and price spikes — but in a slower, more structural way. Central banks are not raiding COMEX. They are accumulating gold through OTC markets, bilateral deals, and direct purchases from refiners. But every ounce they buy and vault privately is an ounce that is *not* available for the system. The total pool of freely available gold contracts. Over time, this constrains the system's ability to honor par.

The causal chain is not "central banks demand COMEX delivery → vaults drain." It is "central banks accumulate physical gold through all channels → freely available supply contracts → the system that depends on that supply for par integrity comes under pressure." The vault drain is a symptom, not the cause. The cause is a sovereign-level shift in reserve composition, triggered by the demonstration that inside money can be frozen.

## V. The Bridge That Vanished

The Exchange for Physical — EFP — is the mechanism connecting the COMEX futures market in New York to the LBMA physical market in London. It is the bridge between paper and physical: a futures holder swaps their contract for London metal, keeping the two markets aligned through arbitrage.

EFP volume has declined sharply. From 362,049 EFPs in March 2020 to 30,000–40,000 per month in 2025 — roughly a 90% decline. Some individual days in 2025 saw fewer than 300 EFPs.

The decline has a proximate cause: regulatory scrutiny and the narrowing of arbitrage opportunities. Between 2017 and 2020, EFPs were used aggressively by bank trading desks — sometimes to manage inventory, sometimes to suppress price discovery. When the mechanism was abused, volume was artificially inflated. The 2025 levels may represent a normalization rather than a breakdown.

But normalization does not explain everything. What it does not explain is why the spot-futures spread widened from a historical $2–5 to $30–50. If the EFP mechanism were functioning normally — if the arbitrage bridge were intact — such spreads would be closed almost immediately by traders swapping futures for physical and pocketing the difference. The fact that the spread persists means the arbitrage is not happening. Either the physical metal is not available in the quantities needed to close the gap, or the delivery timeline has stretched to the point where the arbitrage is no longer profitable.

The spread is the signal. EFP volume is the mechanism. The mechanism has declined. The spread has widened. Whether the EFP decline is normalization or breakdown, the spread tells you the two markets are not converging. They are drifting.

## VI. The Hierarchy Fractures From the Base

The conventional narrative about de-dollarization focuses on the top of the hierarchy: reserve currency status. Will the yuan replace the dollar? Will the euro reclaim share? Will a BRICS currency emerge? These questions assume the break happens at the top — that the dollar loses its position as the world's reserve currency, and something else takes its place.

The vault drain tells a different story. The break is not happening at the top. It is happening at the bottom.

Mehrling's hierarchy places gold at the base, then currency, then deposits, then securities. The dollar's reserve status sits near the top — the layer where international settlement occurs, where reserves are held, where the system's highest-level transactions clear. But the hierarchy is not breaking from the top down. It is fracturing from the bottom up.

Central banks are not replacing dollar reserves with yuan reserves. The yuan's share of global reserves has actually *declined* — from 2.8% in 2022 to 1.9% in 2025. They are replacing *paper claims* with *physical metal*. They are moving down the hierarchy, from securities and deposits (inside money) to gold and commodities (outside money). The reserve asset is not changing — the *nature* of the reserve is changing.

This is what the vault drain actually represents. Not a vote against the dollar. A vote against paper. When the institution that holds your reserves can freeze them, the rational response is not to find a different institution. It is to hold the asset yourself. Gold in your vault cannot be frozen by Washington, Beijing, or Brussels. It is the only reserve asset with no counterparty risk.

The hierarchy is reconstituting. Not collapsing — reorganizing. The top layer (dollar settlement) is still in place. The base layer (physical metal, no counterparty) is growing. The middle layers (paper claims, institutional promises) are thinning. What emerges is not a new hierarchy. It is the old hierarchy with a heavier base and a thinner middle.

The paper price still moves. The screens still tick. The analysts still debate whether gold is "overbought" or "fairly valued." But beneath the screens, the vaults are emptying. The bridge between paper and physical has stopped carrying enough traffic. The participants who used to accept promises are asking for metal.

The hierarchy does not break with a headline. It fractures from the base, quietly, one delivery at a time. By the time the top moves, the base has already shifted.

The guarantor became the source. The vault drain is what the source looks like from below.
