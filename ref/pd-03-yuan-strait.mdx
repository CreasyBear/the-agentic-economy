## I. The Visibility Tax

Kenneth Rogoff, the Harvard economist and former IMF chief economist, identifies three pillars of the dollar's international role: security-based hard power, institutional credibility, and Federal Reserve independence. There is a fourth element that underpins all three — one that rarely appears in reserve currency discussions but that Rogoff has described as perhaps the most consequential: financial surveillance.

Because the vast majority of cross-border payments are routed through US-controlled banking infrastructure — SWIFT messages pass through US correspondent banks, dollar-denominated transactions clear through New York — American authorities can track, freeze, or block assets virtually anywhere in the world. Rogoff calls this "a big part of our spycraft." The dollar's ubiquity is not just an economic advantage. It is an intelligence platform.

The implications are rarely stated plainly. When a country trades in dollars, it is not merely using a currency. It is submitting its transaction data to the United States. When a country builds an alternative clearing system, it is not merely seeking financial independence. It is building intelligence independence.

This is the context in which CIPS, the Kunlun loop, and mBridge must be understood. They are not just payment alternatives. They are visibility reductions. Every transaction that clears through CIPS instead of SWIFT is a transaction Washington cannot see. Every settlement that bypasses the correspondent banking system is a data point removed from the American intelligence apparatus. The plumbing does not just move money. It moves information out of sight.

## II. The First Layer: CIPS

China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System launched in 2015 with a modest scope: clear人民币 transactions between Chinese and foreign banks. It was an efficiency play — a way to settle cross-border人民币 payments without routing through correspondent banks in New York or London.

By 2025, it processed $245 trillion. Direct participants grew from 139 to 193 banks — a 40% increase since 2024. The system handles人民币 cross-border settlement, clearing, and liquidity management.

The scale gap with SWIFT is real. SWIFT processes roughly $7.5 quadrillion annually. CIPS at $245 trillion is approximately 3% of SWIFT's volume. Anyone looking at this ratio and concluding "CIPS is irrelevant" is asking the wrong question.

The right question is: what happens when SWIFT is weaponized?

When the US expelled Russian banks from SWIFT in February 2022, it demonstrated that the dollar clearing system could be used as a coercive instrument. The immediate impact on Russia was severe. The secondary impact on every other non-allied central bank was calculative: if the US can cut off Russia, it can cut off anyone. The value of an alternative clearing system is not proportional to its current volume. It is proportional to the *probability that the primary system will be closed to you*. As that probability increases — and the Russian precedent increased it for every non-Western state — the value of CIPS rises even without a single additional transaction.

CIPS does not need to match SWIFT's volume to be strategically significant. It needs to exist. A road that bypasses a toll bridge does not need to carry the same traffic to be valuable. It just needs to be passable when the bridge is closed.

## III. The Second Layer: Kunlun

CIPS is the clearing system. The Kunlun loop is what runs through it.

Kunlun Bank — a small Chinese bank designated by the US Treasury in 2012 for processing Iranian oil payments — became the node in a loop that has settled 1.3–1.4 million barrels per day of Iranian oil in yuan for over a decade. The mechanism is straightforward: Chinese buyers pay Kunlun in yuan. Kunlun converts to rials or dirhams. Iranian sellers receive payment without touching the dollar system.

The Kunlun loop was not built for the March 2026 crisis. It was built for sanctions evasion — a way to keep Iranian oil flowing after the US sanctioned Iran's central bank. For fourteen years, it operated quietly, below the threshold of systemic significance. It was a workaround, not an architecture.

But workarounds become architectures when the system they work around is no longer available. When Hormuz closed and the IRGC conditioned passage on non-dollar settlement, the Kunlun loop was already operational. The intermediaries existed. The clearing relationships were established. The conversion mechanisms — yuan to rials, yuan to dirhams — had been tested across thousands of transactions over fourteen years.

The crisis did not require the plumbing to be built. It required the plumbing to be *scaled*. And scaling a system that already works is a fundamentally different problem than building one from scratch.

This is the pattern that repeats across every layer of the alternative infrastructure. Each piece was built for a specific, limited purpose — sanctions evasion, transaction efficiency, diplomatic hedging. None was built as a "dollar replacement." But together, they form a system that can function when the dollar system is unavailable. The pieces were not designed to connect. The crisis connected them.

## IV. The Third Layer: mBridge

If CIPS is the alternative road and Kunlun is the traffic that runs on it, mBridge is the highway being built alongside — not yet carrying commercial traffic, but engineered for a fundamentally different kind of travel.

mBridge is the multi-central-bank digital currency platform led by the BIS Innovation Hub, the People's Bank of China, the Hong Kong Monetary Authority, the Bank of Thailand, and the Central Bank of the UAE. It has processed $55 billion in transactions, 95% settled in digital yuan. Its architecture is what distinguishes it from everything that came before.

CIPS clears through traditional banking rails — banks hold accounts, messages pass between institutions, settlement occurs through the banking system. The Kunlun loop relies on correspondent banking relationships — one bank converts for another. Both systems reduce visibility to Washington, but they still operate within a banking framework. mBridge eliminates the banking layer entirely.

Wholesale central bank digital currencies settle directly between central banks. No correspondent bank in the chain. No SWIFT message. No banking intermediary that might be subject to US jurisdiction or pressure. The transaction is peer-to-peer between sovereign monetary authorities, denominated in digital currencies that exist outside the traditional correspondent banking architecture.

mBridge's $55 billion is, in global settlement terms, trivial. Its pilot status means it is years from commercial deployment. But its *architecture* is the endgame of the plumbing story. CIPS reduces visibility. mBridge eliminates it. CIPS bypasses SWIFT. mBridge bypasses the entire banking layer that SWIFT operates within.

The infrastructure is being built in layers — clearing first (CIPS), then bilateral settlement (Kunlun), then direct sovereign settlement (mBridge). Each layer removes an intermediary. Each removal reduces visibility to Washington. The sequence is not accidental. It is architectural.

## V. The Transaction That Proved It

On March 14, 2026, CNN reported that Iran was conditioning passage through the Strait of Hormuz on yuan settlement. On March 22, Lloyd's List confirmed the first verified transaction: the *Newvoyager*, a Chinese-owned, Panama-flagged tanker, paid a toll in yuan — brokered through a Chinese maritime services company — for passage through the strait.

One ship. One toll. One day. The transaction did not end dollar settlement at Hormuz. It did not establish yuan as the currency of the strait.

What it did was prove the system works end-to-end. CIPS cleared the transaction. The Kunlun intermediaries brokered the conversion. The IRGC — a military organization with no particular affinity for financial innovation — accepted yuan because the infrastructure made acceptance easy. The rail was already built. The crisis pointed traffic onto it.

Indian refiners scrambled in the aftermath, settling 60 million barrels in March through yuan and dirhams. The bypass was not theoretical. It was operational.

But one transaction does not make a system. The IRGC's acceptance of yuan could be tactical — a way to extract revenue during a crisis, not a permanent shift. The Indian refiners' settlements could be temporary workarounds. The *Newvoyager* proved the rail *can* carry traffic. It did not prove the traffic *will* continue at scale.

This is the honest limit. The plumbing works. Whether it becomes the default — rather than the emergency alternative — depends on decisions not yet made by actors not yet committed. The infrastructure provides optionality. Optionality is not inevitability.

## VI. The Road Before the Crisis

The standard narrative about de-dollarization is policy-driven. BRICS nations issue declarations. Central banks announce reserve diversification. Politicians make speeches about "a new financial architecture." The implication is that the alternative is aspirational — something being discussed, not something that exists.

The plumbing tells a different story. The infrastructure was not built in response to policy declarations. It was built because trade needed it. Chinese banks needed a way to clear人民币 cross-border transactions — so CIPS was built. Iranian oil needed a way to flow despite sanctions — so the Kunlun loop was built. Central banks needed a way to settle directly without intermediary banks — so mBridge was built. Each piece solved a specific, immediate problem. None was designed as part of a grand strategy to displace the dollar.

Perry Mehrling observed the same pattern in the post-2008 global swap network. The network of central bank swap lines that formalized after the crisis was not primarily about political relations between states. It was about commercial relations between national financial systems. The politics followed the plumbing.

This is the pattern that matters. You do not build a road because you know a crisis is coming. You build a road because trade needs it. When the crisis comes, the road is already there. The crisis does not create the alternative. It reveals it.

The Newvoyager did not replace the dollar at Hormuz. It proved that Hormuz can be passed without the dollar. The distinction is everything. One is a claim about dominance. The other is a claim about optionality. Dominance can be debated. Optionality is a fact.

The guarantor struck. The chokepoint closed. And the plumbing that nobody had been watching cleared its first transaction — fifteen years after it was laid.
