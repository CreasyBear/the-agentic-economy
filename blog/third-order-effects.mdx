Everyone sees the oil price spike. Nobody traces the chain to the end.

On March 2, 2026, Iranian missiles struck Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City. The damage was extensive: 17% of LNG export capacity permanently damaged, 14% of helium production capacity destroyed. Brent crude surged from $75 to $114 per barrel within days. Headlines followed: energy crisis, inflation risk, recession fears.

This is first-order thinking. It stops at the visible shock.

The chain does not stop there. Helium is not LNG. It is a byproduct of natural gas refining, extracted at facilities like Ras Laffan. When the facility stops, helium stops. Qatar produces 33% of global helium supply — 63 million cubic meters in 2025. That supply is now constrained. Prices have doubled to tripled. The market shifted from oversupplied to undersupplied overnight.

This is second-order thinking. It sees the immediate cascade. But it still does not see the end.

Helium is irreplaceable in semiconductor manufacturing. Wafer cooling, plasma etching, leak detection — no substitutes exist. TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix all depend on continuous helium supply. South Korea sourced 64.7% of its helium imports from Qatar in 2025. Japan's semiconductor fabs and MRI manufacturing hubs face exposure if the outage persists beyond 60 days. When helium supply tightens, fab throughput tightens. When fab throughput tightens, GPU supply tightens.

This is third-order thinking. It traces the chain through infrastructure. But it still does not reach the reader.

The agentic economy thesis rests on cheap, abundant compute. Autonomous agents require GPUs to run. Data centers require GPUs to scale. If GPU supply tightens, if build timelines extend, if energy costs rise — the thesis slows. Not fails. Slows. The causal chain from a missile hitting a gas facility in Qatar to a decision not to build an AI agent is seven links long. Most analysis stops at link two.

This is the gap: everyone maps the shock. Nobody maps the constraint.

> The chain is plausible, not proven. Plausibility is not a basis for strategic planning. But the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. The chain deserves tracing.

Saad al-Kaabi, CEO of QatarEnergy, said it on March 19: "I never in my wildest dreams would have thought that Qatar would be — Qatar and the region — in such an attack, especially from a brotherly Muslim country in the month of Ramadan, attacking us in this way."

The statement captures the shock. But it does not capture the timeline. Force majeure declarations cover contracts extending five years out. This is legal protection, not engineering assessment. Partial restoration: 6-12 months, optimistic, requires security guarantees. Full capacity restoration: 3-5 years in the worst case. Historical precedent suggests faster recovery — after the 2019 Abqaiq attack, Saudi Arabia restored 5.7 million b/d capacity within weeks.

Consider the logistics. Liquid helium moves in specialized cryogenic ISO containers with 35-48 day usable windows. Transit from Qatar to end users consumes approximately three weeks. Approximately 200 containers are currently constrained in the region, not rotating. The holding time is fixed by physics. The transit time is fixed by geography. Neither can be accelerated.

Airgas, an Air Liquide subsidiary, declared force majeure on March 17. Customers will receive up to half of their normal monthly helium deliveries, plus a $13.50 per hundred cubic feet surcharge. Linde is implementing rationing protocols without public force majeure. Spot prices have doubled. The market is allocating scarcity.

The principal says: I did not anticipate this constraint. The counterparty says: you assumed infinite supply. The fab operator says: I have 60-90 days of inventory, not 30. The AI infrastructure builder says: I have an 18-month build timeline. Everyone is right. No one is prepared for a 12-month disruption.

Semiconductor fabs run on assumptions of continuous supply. TSMC produces 18% of global chips. Samsung and SK Hynix dominate memory. All three depend on helium for wafer cooling and plasma etching. The process cannot substitute. Helium's thermal properties are unique. Its inertness is non-negotiable.

Consider a simple chain. Ras Laffan damage reduces helium output by 14%. South Korean fabs lose 64.7% of their import supply. They draw down inventory. They negotiate with alternative suppliers — US, Algeria, Russia. None can scale rapidly. US produces 35% of global helium but cannot redirect without disrupting domestic customers. Algeria has reserves but limited export infrastructure. Russia has capacity but geopolitical constraints limit immediate expansion.

But the chain has slack. Major fabs maintain 60-90 days of helium inventory, not 30. Samsung has on-site helium recycling systems recovering 80-90% of consumed gas. At 2-3x prices, marginal helium users exit — the balloon industry (less than 5% of consumption) disappears overnight. Research labs defer non-critical work. The market clears.

The constraint is real. It is not catastrophic. Fab throughput declines measurably, not catastrophically. Yield rates drop as cooling becomes less consistent. Plasma etching slows. Quality control tightens. The effect compounds across thousands of wafers.

GPU supply tightens. But not uniformly. NVIDIA's H100, B100, and Rubin chips use TSMC's 4nm and 3nm processes — helium-intensive. But AI inference increasingly uses older nodes: 7nm, 5nm — less helium-dependent. Memory chips (DRAM, NAND) are more exposed than logic chips. The constraint hits specific products, not the entire category.

The agentic economy requires cheap compute. Cheap compute requires abundant GPUs. Abundant GPUs require uninterrupted fab throughput. Uninterrupted fab throughput requires continuous helium supply. The chain is seven links. The constraint propagates at the speed of the slowest link. The slack absorbs some shock. Not all.

The agentic economy thesis rests on a specific assumption: compute costs decline over time. Moore's Law, scaled by hyperscaler purchasing power, by efficiency gains, by competition. The trajectory is downward. Agents become economically viable because the cost of running them falls faster than the value they create.

This assumption is now under pressure. Not broken. Under pressure.

Energy costs are rising. Data centers consume 1,000+ TWh/year globally by 2026. US data centers will consume 8% of national power by 2030. European electricity prices rose from €35/MWh in 2020 to €500/MWh in 2022, moderated, and are rising again. Sixty percent of data center OpEx can be energy-related. The margin pressure is direct.

Helium costs are rising. Spot prices have doubled. Force majeure declarations restrict supply. Alternative suppliers cannot scale rapidly. The constraint is physical, not financial.

GPU availability is tightening. Fab throughput declines. Lead times extend. The build timeline for a data center — 18 months under normal conditions — stretches to 24 months in a sustained disruption scenario. The capital is committed. The return is delayed.

But the thesis does not depend solely on hardware costs. Model efficiency is improving faster than hardware costs rise. Quantization, distillation, sparse architectures — agents run on smaller models, fewer tokens, less compute. TPUs, NPUs, inference-optimized chips deliver 10-100x efficiency gains for agent workloads. The hardware mix shifts.

The thesis does not fail. It slows. The agentic economy does not become impossible. It becomes more expensive. The timeline extends. The actors with capital reserves win. The actors without reserves wait. The actors with efficiency gains adapt.

This is not a prediction. It is an observation of the chain. The missile hit Ras Laffan. Helium supply dropped. Fab throughput faces pressure. GPU supply tightens selectively. AI infrastructure costs rise. The agentic economy slows, adapts, continues.

The gap is not in the chain. The gap is in the visibility.

Economic modelling assumes ceteris paribus — all else equal. Oil prices rise, GDP falls by X. Inflation rises by Y. Central banks respond with Z. The model holds the rest constant.

The chain does not hold the rest constant. The chain propagates. Oil prices rise. Helium supply falls. Semiconductor throughput declines. GPU availability tightens. AI infrastructure costs rise. The agentic economy slows. Each link modifies the next. The model cannot capture this because the model was not built for chains this long.

The links beyond helium are plausible, not proven. No public data shows TSMC reducing throughput due to helium. No earnings calls have flagged this. No industry analysts have reported yield degradation. The chain is hypothesis, not observation. But the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. The disruption is weeks old. The data will lag.

Strategic planning requires longer maps. Not infinite maps. Longer maps. The principal who assumes compute costs will decline must ask: what breaks the assumption? The builder who commits to an 18-month timeline must ask: what extends it? The investor who underwrites an AI infrastructure bet must ask: what variables are not in the model?

This is the third structural gap in the agentic economy. The first was actors without standing. The second was judgment without context. This is planning without visibility. The chain is longer than the map. The map must grow.
